**Description of team diversity (max 350 words)**

Our team – with 100+ cumulative years of expertise in psychiatric epidemiology, population science, clinical medicine, social science, and statistics – brings together a senior mental health researcher (XH) and early career biostatistician (NM), with biostatistics (KB) population science and data curation (KH), and epidemiology (CI) experts, and a Youth Advisory Group, to deliver the project. All Co-Applicants are based at the Africa Health Research Institute (AHRI). The lead applicant (XH) is an expert with lived experience as well as an Associate Professor of Global Health, with expertise in supporting meaningful youth involvement in research through co-creation. She, and AHRI broadly, have longstanding track-records of collaborating with community advisory structures, including Youth Advisory Groups, to deliver high-impact research. In this project, the Youth Advisory Group will be comprised of ten 13-24-year-olds living in the AHRI Health and Demographic Surveillance Site (HDSS). As detailed in the full proposal, the Youth Advisory Group is <u>not for the purpose of primary data collection</u>, but will provide meaningful thought leadership to refine, guide, and nuance the proposed research. Adult members of the HDSS community will also be integrated into the project management structure through AHRI’s Community Advisory Board. The Community Advisory Board will partner with the research team to:

1.  Inform members of the community about the project, and feedback any questions or concerns regarding the research to the project team;

2.  Allow community members beyond the Youth Advisory Group to contribute to the scope of the proposed research through Community Advisory Board meetings; and

3.  Safeguard community voice in the research process by contributing to the ethical and scientific quality, relevance and acceptability of the proposed projects.

We will also invite stakeholders from national and local government departments (Health, Human Settlements, Social Development, and Education) and civil society organisations, non-governmental organisations, and community-based organisations to provide input into the research questions, interpretation of findings, and generation of policy and programmatic implications arising from the findings, including through townhall and networking meetings (see below). The project will also leverage AHRI’s core infrastructure including Biostatistics (led by KB), the Social Science Core, and the Population Science Core (led by KH).

2.  **Proposal summary (max 300 words****)**

Through a process of co-creation and secondary data analysis, we will generate transformative insights into the longitudinal relationship between key social determinants and symptoms of depression among South African young people. We will create and analyse the CO-LUMINATE dataset which links existing data on depressed mood and behavioural mediators of depressed mood at multiple time points from **5279 adolescents aged 13-24-years**, with data about their exposure, over the life course, to social determinants of interest, from their households’ Health and Demographic Surveillance system (HDSS) data. A Youth Advisory Group (YAG) comprised of ten 13-24-year-olds living in the HDSS will refine, guide, and nuance the proposed research, through a process of sustained co-creation. Our objectives include to:

1.  describe patterns in CO-LUMINATE young people’s exposure to social determinants of interest (low socioeconomic status (SES), food insecurity, and caregiver co-residency);

2.  explore interactions between social determinants and behavioural mediators of depression;

3.  describe prevalence of depression in the CO-LUMINATE dataset;

4.  assess the impact of social determinants on depression among young people, with a focus on examining longitudinal relationships and the role of behavioural mediators; and

5.  co-create a shared understanding of the role of social determinants in young people’s mental health, through engaging a YAG.

The intended impacts of this project are to:

1.  transform the way in which data-driven research is done, centring young people in co-created research which concerns them;

2.  generate a valuable data resource for longitudinal analysis to understand young people’s mental health in African countries; and

<!-- -->

4.  generate new knowledge regarding the role and mechanisms of influence of social determinants in depression among young people; and

5.  highlight potential areas for intervention to address depression in young people.

Our approach has the potential to be scaled by leveraging South African (South African Population Research Infrastructure Network – SAPRIN) and continental (African Population Cohorts Consortium – APCC) networks to share the approach with other HDSS nodes throughout South Africa and the continent.

**4. Background, problem identification and significance (max 1500 words)**

**4.1 Introduction**

**4.1.1 Context**

About 34% of adolescents globally are at risk of depression\[1\]. Approximately 90% of the world’s population of young people live in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs)\[2\], settings characterised by a host of biological, psychological, social, and environmental stressors which are either unique to resource-constrained settings or amplified in them.

The aetiology of depression has some aspects which are universal, and others which are context-specific\[3\]; but all arise from the interplay between neurobiological and social or environmental factors\[4, 5\].

However, while these facts are well-established, three issues characterise the state of knowledge in relation to youth mental health:

1.  Much of the evidence about depression among young people was not generated in LMICs;

2.  Among the possible determinants and mechanisms of depression, the least attention has been paid to social and environmental factors (broadly, ‘social determinants’), yet stressors in these domains – for instance low socioeconomic status (SES), food insecurity, and irregular patterns of caregiver residence with their children – predominate in LMICs; and

3.  Attempts to understand the complex relationships between social determinants and young people’s depression often favour empirical insights over lived experience expertise, perpetuating power imbalances and increasing distance between young people, and the science which seeks to know them.

**4.1.2 Proposed Solution**

This study will generate data-driven insights into the longitudinal relationship between key social determinants and symptoms of depression among South African young people, all the while foregrounding young people’s lived experience expertise through a co-created research agenda and process. Our solution has two parts:

**4.1.3 Part 1: Youth Co-Creation**

A Youth Advisory Group (YAG) comprised of approximately ten 13-24-year-olds living in the AHRI Somkhele HDSS communities will provide meaningful thought leadership to refine, guide, and nuance the proposed research. They will:

1.  produce photo diaries to inform the research’s conceptualisation of social determinants and their influence on young people’s mental health;

2.  provide input into the proposed analyses by producing lay directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) based on group narrative-building around the photo diaries;

3.  support results’ interpretation through workshopping the findings; and

4.  be platformed, during a photo diary exhibition townhall dissemination event, to support dissemination of the findings in their own words and in relation to their photography.

**4.1.4 Part 2: CO-LUMINATE Secondary Data Analysis**

The data-driven aspect of this project entails the creation of the CO-LUMINATE longitudinal linked dataset. This dataset will allow us to understand the longitudinal relationships between SES, food security, and caregiver residency and co-residency, and depressed mood (measured at multiple time points), among rural South African young people aged 13-24, and use these findings to generate and test hypotheses regarding the mechanisms underpinning young people’s depression in LMICs.

The CO-LUMINATE longitudinal dataset will link existing data from 5279 young people aged 13-24 years for whom we have rigorous measures of depressed mood at multiple time points from AHRI’s past research studies, with their households’ HDSS data. Through this process, each young person’s depression data will be linked with an extensive longitudinal database with information regarding their household’s SES and food security. The HDSS also allows us to link young people to their caregivers, so that we can examine patterns of co-residence between a young person’s caregivers, and between them and their caregivers.

The novel contribution of this project, and our ability to do this innovative work, arises from three facts:

1.  AHRI’s HDSS, like most, does not include rigorous individual-level measurement of young people's mental health outcomes, but offers an incredible opportunity to examine social exposures longitudinally;

2.  Over the past decade, AHRI has conducted cohort studies among young people which included rigorous measurement of depressed mood (SSQ-14 and PHQ-9) at one or more time points, providing a unique data resource;

3.  by bringing these two types of data together, we will be able to examine – in a sample of 5279 – patterns of exposure to social determinants and trajectories of young people’s mental health (with a focus on depression).

The cohort studies that we propose to use for this project – *DREAMS, Multilevel, Isisekelo Sempilo,* and *Treatment as Prevention –* were nested within the HDSS, allowing us to link young people’s individual-level depression data gathered in these studies with their household’s HDSS data, creating an unprecedented longitudinal dataset of household social exposures and depression in young people.

The basis for this project has already begun: in partnership with Aga Khan University, KEMRI, the University of Michigan Center for Global Health Equity, Co-Applicant NM integrated data from the nested studies into a harmonised individual-level mental health dataset for pooled analyses. 

Now, the harmonised individual-level mental health dataset can be linked to the study participants’ household HDSS data, allowing for longitudinal measures of young people’s social and environmental exposures at the household level to be explored in relation to their depression symptoms.

This opportunity will allow us to achieve our aim to advance understanding of the longitudinal relationships between social and environmental exposures and depression in young people in under-resourced settings.

**4.2 Significance of the Project: Purpose and Anticipated Results **

In service of this aim, the following objectives and research questions will guide this work:

Objective 1: To describe patterns in CO-LUMINATE young people’s exposure to social determinants of interest (low SES, food insecurity, and caregiver co-residency).

> <u>Research question 1.1</u> What is the prevalence of low SES over the life course of CO-LUMINATE participants, and what patterns can be seen?
>
> <u>Research question 1.2</u> What is the prevalence of food insecurity over the life course of CO-LUMINATE participants, and what patterns can be seen?
>
> <u>Research question 1.3</u> What do patterns of caregiver co-residency with CO-LUMINATE participants look like?

Objective 2: To explore interactions between social determinants and behavioural mediators of depression.

> <u>Research question 2.1</u> What are the correlations between the three social determinants of interest (low SES, food insecurity, and caregiver co-residency) and other social factors (including migration, substance use, and sexual behaviour)?
>
> <u>Research question 2.2</u> What are the correlations between low SES, food insecurity, and caregiver co-residency and young people’s behaviours including migration, substance use, and sexual behaviour?

Objective 3: To describe prevalence of depression in the CO-LUMINATE dataset.

> <u>Research question 3.1</u> What is the performance and accuracy of the SSQ-14 compared with PHQ-9?
>
> <u>Research question 3.2</u> What are age-appropriate SSQ-14 cutoffs for identifying depression?
>
> <u>Research question 3.3</u> What is the percentage of individuals screening positive based on these cutoffs?

Objective 4: To assess the impact of social determinants on depression among young people, with a focus on examining longitudinal relationships and the role of behavioural mediators.

> <u>Research question 4.1</u> How are social determinants associated longitudinally with depression among young people?
>
> <u>Research question 4.2</u> What are the causal pathways between low SES, food security, or caregiver co-residency and depression?
>
> <u>Research question 4.3</u> Are relationships between social determinants and depression mediated by behavioural factors (measured at individual-level) such as migration, substance use or sexual behaviour?

Objective 5: To co-create a shared understanding of the role of social determinants in young people’s mental health, through engaging a youth advisory group.

> <u>Research question 5.1</u> What are good practices for co-creating a data-driven research agenda with young people living in an under-resourced setting?

This work will advance established knowledge by providing a precedent for co-creating data-driven research agendas with young people in respect of large datasets, and improving our understanding of the longitudinal relationship – including the role and influence of time-varying exposures – between key social determinants and depression in young people in Africa.

In the latter respect, our proposed analyses will significantly contribute to our understanding of young people’s mental health by addressing these gaps:

1.  Food security status: Longitudinal data on adults\[6\] and cross-sectional data on adolescent\[7\] suggests that food insecurity is associated with depressive symptoms. However, longitudinal data on young people, and data addressing multiple individual-level mediators are lacking.

2.  SES: There has been a lot of research on the relationship between low SES and adolescents’ depressive symptoms, but little is known about the mediating mechanisms, nor about which longitudinal patterns of exposure to low SES are most predictive of depression\[8\].

3.  Caregiver co-residency: The use of large datasets to quantify whether a young person’s pattern of living with one or more caregivers, and how this has changed over the course of their life, influences mental health, is rare\[9\].

The potential findings have concrete policy implications. For instance, SES and food security have clear associated policy provisions to address them (including, for instance, social protection, school feeding schemes etc.). Caregiver residency and co-residency, can inform a range of programming which is responsive to young people’s varied caregiver residence arrangements. Our methods will also be highly adaptable, because the AHRI HDSS is one of six within South Africa that are part of the SAPRIN network (director KH), and many of those have nested cohorts similar to those represented in the harmonised dataset of mental health measures. Furthermore, AHRI co-leads (co-PI KH) the African Population Cohorts Consortium (APCC) – a network of 98 HDSS sites across 23 African countries – which would allow the transportation of this method across the continent.

5\. Project design and methodology (max 1500 words)

The proposed work will be achieved through two complementary parts:

1.  Part one: co-creating a shared research project to understand young people’s lived experience of social determinants and mechanisms of mental health. <u>This is not for the purposes of primary data collection</u>, but to allow for a youth-led research agenda and framework for making meaning in Part two.

2.  Part two: creating the CO-LUMINATE longitudinal linked dataset, and analysing it.

In the sections below, we describe the process by which a YAG will be involved to ensure that every aspect of the project – from proposing analyses to interpreting findings and generating hypotheses for future testing – is subject to ground-truthing by young people from the study communities. We also describe the novelty, magnitude, and potential of a linked dataset of 5279 young people aged 13-24 years, and how we intend to analyse it to generate data-driven insights tailored to the priorities of our population.

**5.1. Study setting and design**

**5.1.1 Study setting**

AHRI is a transdisciplinary scientific research institute in KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa. AHRI’s Somkhele campus is located in an under-resourced rural area centred in a Health and Demographic Surveillance System (HDSS), which conducts tri-annual surveys of all 22,000 households, and annual surveys of 150,000 individuals in an area of 845 km².. The area typifies the challenges that face young people in LMICs: poverty, food insecurity, high HIV prevalence, migration, and substance abuse, all increasing vulnerability to depression.

**5.1.2 Study Design and Participants**

**5.1.2.1 Part 1: Youth Co-Creation**

The YAG will be comprised of ten 13-24-year-olds living within the AHRI HDSS. The YAG will provide meaningful thought leadership to refine, guide, and nuance the proposed research.

**5.1.2.2 Part 2: CO-LUMINATE Secondary Data Analysis**

The linked dataset that will be used for this project is from studies which included young people living in the AHRI HDSS, and measured depression at multiple time points (Table 1). Data from these nested studies have already been brought together in a harmonised dataset of mental health measures. Under CO-LUMINATE, they will be merged with HDSS data using either unique individual and household identifiers.

**5.2 Procedure, Data, and Sampling**

***5.2.1 Youth-Co-Creation***

***5.2.2.1 Co-creation phase 1 (Photo diaries of social determinants)***

Following an inception workshop, YAG members will be provided with affordable digital cameras and asked to record photo diaries to inform the research’s conceptualisation of social determinants and their influence on young people’s mental health. This activity will be guided by Good Clinical Practice and Child Safeguarding Principles.

***5.2.2.2 Co-creation phase 2 (Group narrative-building around social determinants photos and production of a lay DAG)***

The YAG will provide input into the proposed analyses by producing a lay Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) based on group narrative-building around the photo diaries. The DAG will be co-developed with AHRI researchers, and fed back into the analysis plan. The YAG’s developing understanding of the relationship between social determinants and depression, expressed through their photos, narratives, and lay DAG, will generate hypotheses regarding the mechanisms underpinning young people’s depression in LMICs.

***521.2.3 Interpretation of findings workshop***

The YAG will engage in a findings workshop, during which the findings from the CO-LUMINATE data analyses will be presented to them for interpretation and reflection.

***5.2.2.4 Curation and presentation of a photo diary exhibition townhall dissemination event***

The DAG, photos, and research findings (presented in infographic flyers) will be shared with key stakeholders during a photo diary exhibition townhall event. Young people from the YAG will be platformed to support dissemination of the findings during this event.

***5.2.2* CO-LUMINATE Secondary Data Analysis**

***5.2.2.1 Study Designs and Sampling***

*The DREAMS and Multilevel cohorts* were part of the impact evaluation of the DREAMS Partnership, a multi-component HIV prevention intervention aimed at biological, behavioural, social and structural sources of HIV acquisition risk. *Isisekelo Sempilo* was a trial of the effectiveness of a sexual and reproductive health (SRH) intervention, combined with peer navigator support, to reduce transmissible HIV amongst young people. The *HIV Treatment as Prevention (TasP)* cluster-randomised trial implemented six-monthly repeat home-based HIV counselling and testing and referral to study clinics. Sampling techniques used to select participants for these studies are outlined in Table 1 .

***5.2.2.2** **Data management and analysis***

As noted, data from these studies have already been integrated into the harmonised dataset of mental health measures, and the depression measures from each study have been transformed to a common format, curated and stored in AHRI’s data repository, using the Julia Programming language. Similarly, all HDSS data are cleaned, combined and organised using Julia, curated and stored in AHRI’s data repository. For CO-LUMINATE, we will link the harmonised dataset of mental health measures from 5279 young people with their HDSS data to do the following analyses:

**A. Describing social determinants of depression:** We will use longitudinal HDSS data to describe patterns of young people’s exposure to social determinants of interest (low socioeconomic status, food insecurity, and caregiver co-residency) over time. We will use methods such as longitudinal latent class analysis to identify distinct trajectories/patterns of social determinants, which can then be explored in relationship to later mental health outcomes.

**B. Exploring interactions between social determinants and possible mediators:** Mixed effects regression models (accounting for repeated observations on individuals) will be used to examine the relationships between social determinants. For example, we will investigate whether caregiver residency and co-residency is associated with characteristics such as food insecurity and socioeconomic status. We will also look at relationships with mediators of interest, such migration, substance use or sexual behaviour (measured at the individual-level and captured in the harmonised dataset of mental health measures).

**C. Estimating the prevalence of depression:** Prior to estimating depression prevalence, we will evaluate the performance and accuracy of the SSQ-14 compared with PHQ-9. Analysis will be stratified by age groups (13-19, 20-24) to determine age-appropriate cutoffs for identifying depression. These cutoffs will subsequently be used to determine depression prevalence at each wave of longitudinal data collection.

**D. Assessing the impact of social determinants on depression:** Logistic regression models (adjusted for potential confounders) will be used to investigate whether social factors are associated with depression among adolescents and young adults. Various combinations of social factor exposures will also be explored. The association of latent subgroups identified in step A with depression may also be investigated. Social factors identified as being associated with depression will be included as exposure variables in causal analysis. Prior to estimating the causal effect, we will use the co-created DAGs to illustrate the hypothesized causal relationships between exposure and depression. The G-computation method, which accounts for time-varying exposures, will be used to estimate the impact of social factors on depression. Additionally, this method will assess whether these effects are mediated by behavioural factors (measured at individual-level) such as migration, substance use or sexual behaviour.
